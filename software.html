The Problem

When agents write the code, the constraint moves to the people reviewing it.

The bottleneck used to be developer throughput — features written, bugs fixed, PRs merged. When agents take that work, the bottleneck shifts. It becomes developer judgement on what agents produce: architectural decisions, integration choices, edge cases that weren't in the spec.

The teams that get into trouble aren't the ones that adopted AI coding too slowly. They're the ones that adopted it without managing the WIP explosion that followed.

The Framework

Automate. Delegate. Accelerate. The same sequence applies to software.

The agent era arrives in three moves in software, just as in logistics. Each one raises the demands on the humans managing the output.

Step 1

Automate

Strip out the rule-based work — boilerplate, tests, documentation, code review scaffolding. Without clean workflows and clear task definitions, agents produce output nobody can safely integrate.

Step 2

Delegate

Hand well-scoped, bounded tasks to AI coding agents. Humans review completions at defined checkpoints — not continuously supervising every line. PACE manages the handoff points and buffers the review queue.

Step 3

Accelerate

As agents take the implementation work, your senior engineers shift from writing code to managing the constraints agents surface. The demand on judgement goes up, not down. BBIT gives your team the thinking skills to handle it.

PACE

Flow management for software teams. The same discipline as PAVE, built for your context.

PACE applies Theory of Constraints to software development. It makes work visible, surfaces the constraint, and tells your team what to work on next — not what's been sitting in the backlog longest.

When AI coding agents enter the system, PACE manages the handoff points: what goes to an agent, what comes back to a human, and how to keep the review queue from becoming the new bottleneck.

The Risk

The expert bottleneck is the most dangerous failure mode in AI-assisted development.

Here's the pattern: agents generate faster than seniors can review. The queue grows. Seniors context-switch to manage the backlog. Their throughput drops. The team adds more agents to compensate. The queue grows faster.

The fix isn't fewer agents. It's managing the constraint. Protect the expert reviewers. Define what goes to them and what doesn't. Buffer the handoff. PACE is how that's done in practice.

Black Belt in Thinking

The thinking skills the agent era demands from your senior engineers.

When agents write the implementation, the value of your engineers lies entirely in their judgement: diagnosing problems, identifying constraints, deciding what the agent got wrong and why. These are higher-order skills than writing code, and they need to be trained deliberately.

BBIT — Black Belt in Thinking — is that training. Built on the same Theory of Constraints thinking that PACE is built on, deployed across organisations managing complex operations under pressure.
